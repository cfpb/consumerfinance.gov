Did you know?
  • You don’t have to take out the full amount of loans offered to you. Only borrow the amount you need.
  • Because student debt is permanent, you should not use student loans to cover costs outside of your education. (For example, you can use student loan funds to pay for childcare but not to buy a car.)
  • The total amount of federal loans you’re allowed to borrow goes up each year you are in school.
  • Paying down the interest on unsubsidized loans while you’re in school will save you money in the long run.
  • Federal loans do not provide a government guarantee of the quality of the school. Federal loans do not guarantee that you will get a good job when you graduate.
  • You have to pay back your loans even if you don’t graduate.

How do student loans work?

Loans are money you have to pay back with interest. That means you'll pay more than you originally borrowed. To save money on the cost of student loans, you have three options: borrow less, borrow at a lower interest rate, or take less time to repay your loans by making bigger monthly payments. (As the graph below shows, you’ll pay far more interest over 25 years than over 10.)

GRAPH

So why take out student loans? A degree can open up more career options and increase your future earnings. If higher education helps you reach your goals, the cost of student loans may be a good investment in your future—if you can afford to repay them.

But be aware of the risks and tradeoffs:

  • You will owe monthly payments for many years. The average repayment period for the typical amount of debt is 20 years. These payments will take up money in your budget that you won’t be able to spend on other things.
  • Missing payments can keep you from other goals. If you can’t afford your payments and miss a payment deadline, your credit score might go down. Lower credit scores can make it harder to get approved for a credit card or borrow money to buy a car or house. Lower credit scores can also result in higher interest rates and make borrowing money later more expensive.
  • Repaying student loans is the only way you’re guaranteed to get rid of them. While loan forgiveness programs do exist for federal loans, it's not guaranteed. Even declaring bankruptcy usually doesn't dissolve student debt.

Get your uncovered costs down to $0

From cheapest to most expensive, here are your options for closing that gap:

  • Search for additional grants and scholarships: Explore whether your state or local community offers scholarships or grants. Contact your high school counselor to see if they have any additional leads.
  • Reduce costs: Can you realistically spend less on indirect costs? For example, can you live at home or buy a refurbished laptop?
  • Find more cash: Can you contribute more from savings or work?
  • Talk to the financial aid office: Does this school offer a low- or no-interest tuition payment plan? You and your family might be able to budget for paying your tuition gradually over time.
  • Federal loans: Your next cheapest option is federal Direct Subsidized Loans, if you’re allowed to borrow any more, followed by Direct Unsubsidized Loans. Generally, your financial aid offer will provide you the maximum amount of Direct Loans available to you but contact your financial aid office to find out more.
  • Last resort borrowing: If you still have uncovered costs, consider applying for a private student loan from a bank or credit union or asking your parents to take out a federal Parent PLUS loan.

Covering upfront costs

Getting your funding gap to $0 is a big step, but you need to make sure you’ll have the right amount of money at the right time. This page will check that you’ll be prepared to cover the large direct costs due at the start of each term.

Direct costs, like tuition, fees, and on-campus housing and meals, must be paid to the school at the start of each term (aka semester at most schools). Unless you directly get a check, most grants, scholarships, and loans are distributed through the school at that time. After the school takes out money for these charges, you will get a bill if you owe a remaining balance. If there’s money left over, you will receive a check (or direct deposit) of the remaining funds.

This estimate does NOT include:

  • Indirect costs for other items you might need, like computers, childcare, or school supplies
  • Your personal and family contribution—you may need to put that cash towards indirect costs
  • Money from a work-study or off-campus job—you have to earn it first
