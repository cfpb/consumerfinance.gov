Planning how you’ll get to work

Getting to work on time is important for keeping your job. Having a plan and a backup option is a good idea in case you get into a bind. We'll help you figure out your best option for getting to work, how to budget for it, and what to do if you need a backup plan.

Meet Mya and Frankie. They’ll help you think about your plan.

Mya plans to bike to work

Mya decided to bike to work because there’s a bikeshare near her school. Bikeshares allow you to borrow a bike from different self-service bike stations around the city. Mya heads straight to work from class — some days she has time to walk, but usually she’s in a rush and biking is faster.

Mya’s long-term goal

Mya wants to buy an annual bikeshare membership since her youth employment program offers a special rate and it’d be a better deal than paying per ride. The $60 annual membership works out to a little over $1 per week (over 52 weeks in a year). In order to save money for this, Mya will walk to work two days a week for the next few months.

Frankie plans to take the bus

Frankie doesn’t live near public transportation, so he usually gets a ride to the closest bus stop. The bus ride is about 25 minutes long, so he leaves his house at 7:10 a.m. to allow enough time to get dropped off, ride the bus, and arrive at work before his 8 a.m. shift starts.

Frankie’s long-term goal

Frankie will take the bus five days a week, but over the next year, he will save up to buy a car so he doesn’t have to rely on friends and family for rides to the bus stop. While he's saving up for a car, he will buy a $50 monthly bus pass because it’s more cost-effective than paying $2 each way for a bus ticket.

Now let's think through your long-term goal.

Figuring out your long-term goal may help you figure out the best way to get to work now while you’re working towards your goal.
